The Crown Season 4 Episode 1: Gold Stick”

The Crown's doomed romance begins with a tragic twist

23 December 2020, by Carson Zhang

Next Episode >>>

[Spoilers for the season four premiere of The Crown, "Gold Stick"]

Season premieres of The Crown have never been monumental episodes. They're solid episodes that reintroduce us to the royal family with a new seasonal arc in mind. That's perfectly fine, but like season three's "Olding" before it, there's a sort of ambivalence amidst all the drama as the show returns for its fourth season. Just like the plot where the Palace's art historian was secretly a Soviet spy, "Gold Stick" throws some narrative curveballs that lack the momentum to really land. It's not for lack of trying by the cast and crew: O'Connor and Menzies are especially good in the episode, and newcomers Gillian Anderson and Emma Corrin as Thatcher and Diana immediately integrated themselves as invaluable members of The Crown's formidable ensemble. Episode director Benjamin Caron is perhaps the best television director right now, and the production quality is out of this world. However, The Crown's very structure makes the episode's twist an incredibly hard sell.

Dickie Mountbatten is a familiar character to the series, but he's only made a handful of notable appearances over the years. They weren't always endearing, either; the most prominent memory of him I can think of is his attempted coup back in season three. His fatherly relationship with Charles was frequently discussed, but rarely shown before "Gold Stick": O'Connor's grieving performance is fantastic, but it's hard to share Charles' sentiments about Mountbatten's death. As I've said previously, The Crown is far too scattered to be a great serial drama. Mountbatten's assassination leading to Charles' courtship of Diana is a good idea, but it lacks impact without enough context or investment in Charles' relationships with Camilla and Mountbatten.

Of course, Morgan does try to remedy that issue with an egregiously expository monologue from Philip to Charles in which he helpfully explains (in extensive detail) both his and Charles' relationship with the father figure they shared. Tobias Menzies is simply extraordinary, his casting being one of the best choices of the past two seasons; his tenuously vulnerable performance distracts from how obvious the writing is, and yet there's an intimidating edge in his tenderness. With his performance alone, Menzies implicitly explains Charles and Philip's difficulty connecting over the years. Philip has grown a great deal over the years, but he's still unwilling to accept that he might've been neglecting Charles like his own father neglected him. On the other hand, the writing—so frightfully afraid that the audience might miss its obvious message—only reinforces the fact Morgan isn't really suited to serial drama as Menzies parrots himself for minutes.

Other aspects of the show compensate for Morgan's inconsistencies; Caron builds up to Mountbatten's assassination with frightfully anxious tension in the show's most overtly stylistic scene to date. Whether you've read up and know he's doomed or the IRA's threat is lingering in your mind, the threat of danger rises with heartpounding intensity as one of the family's three hunting parties becomes an unseen assailant's prey. The swift cuts between Charles, Mountbatten, and Anne—punctuated by the sharp sounds of chopping and gunfire—builds to a haunting climax as the Mountbatten boat erupts in flames. Charles' reaction to the news of his uncle's death is equally clever; meshed into the scenery of a wide shot, the Prince of Wales howls with grief in a gorgeous meadow. A larger-than-life prince writ small, suffering Irish cruelty in a radiant Irish field, a miserable and lonely man despite his idyllic surroundings; it's the perfect representation of Charles' pain.

Caron handles the happier tones of the episode with equal excellence. The dreamy introduction of Charles and Diana is undeniably charming, and instantly unforgettable for the prince: even the future ruler of Britain doesn't often encounter a shy, mad tree from A Midsummer Night's Dream. The scene is a classic meet-cute, but it doesn't forget to hammer in some key details that will lead the pair's relationship to ruin. For one, Charles is a middle-aged artistic snob while Diana is a sixteen-year-old just trying to have a good time—he's looking for a suitable queen, and she probably needs a date to the prom.

On the other hand, Diana's naivety doesn't imply idiocy; she's committed to the idea of being with Charles and throws everything she has into it. Emma Corrin seems effortlessly endearing as Diana, but the future Prince and Princess of Wales' whimsical first encounter is completely deliberate on her part. Fairytales aren't supposed to be strategic, they're moments of perfect serendipity; Cinderella doesn't plan on losing a glass slipper, it's just a stroke of luck that miraculously brings the prince back to her. The royal family—Dickie most of all—is desperate to construct some fairytale ending for Charles with Diana, but in his mind he's already found it with Camilla. Though everyone will try to force Diana as a fit for Charles, herself included, chance inevitably triumphs over scheming in the laws of fairytales; try as she might to the contrary, Diana will never be anything more than one of Cinderella's scheming step-sisters.

On the subject of fairytale villains, Thatcher is elected as Britain's first woman Prime Minister. With two women in charge, everyone (without prompt) throws in their two cents: Philip and Thatcher's husband Denis doubt the efficacy of two women leading the country, Elizabeth argues the contrary, while Thatcher herself thinks that women are generally too emotional to hold positions of power. The first actual audience between the Queen and Prime Minister is, as expected, much more complicated than either of them expected. Elizabeth doesn't find a kindred spirit in Thatcher, and the Prime Minister, pleasantly satisfied for now, will find her own issues with the Queen quite soon. It's a solid introduction to an uncomfortable dynamic between the two women that will continue throughout the season, and an impressive first appearance by Gillian Anderson. The voice is a bit excessive, almost as exaggerated as her massive wig, but otherwise it's a completely compelling transformation of one of TV's most recognizable stars. (Because of The X-Files for most people, but Hannibal for me. Love that show.)

And... that's pretty much it for the episode. Not that other things don't happen: we simultaneously learn that Anne has married Mark Phillips and that their relationship has soured like milk. Thatcher promises war against the IRA. Charles gets with countless girls, but finds their lack of wedding rings to be disagreeable. Again, Menzies does some heavy lifting in the episode in a tender moment between Philip and Anne (those two are probably my favorites now, especially since Charles is really starting to plummet), but the total lack of context surrounding almost every aspect of the episode leaves it a little lethargic. Besides its generally tepid exposition, the episode's key narrative point falls flat without a proper foundation. With a literally explosive turn, "Gold Stick" should have propelled The Crown into its fourth season. Instead, it feels like the show has started to exhaust itself; on a limp, the show trudges into its biggest season to date.

Episode grade: 3/5

Miscellaneous details:

— "Gold Stick" was directed by Benjamin Caron and written by Peter Morgan.

— The episode's title refers to a ceremonial position held by Lord Mountbatten as bodyguard to the monarch. It's rather fitting, since he spends the episode trying to save the monarchy by deterring Charles from Camilla.

— If the love story of Diana and Charles was set in A Midsummer Night's Dream, I know exactly who would play the ass.

— Peter Morgan and Gillian Anderson were actually dating during this season, and broke up after it released. Probably because he wouldn't stop making her character say that the country needed to change from top to bottom.

— That the show hates Thatcher was inevitable, but I didn't expect Morgan to go after her so transparently: in the episode, she both declares women too emotional to be leaders (besides herself, of course) before then passionately declaring war on the IRA to a stoically grieving Elizabeth. If she's meant to be iron in any way, it's because she's brittle.

— The show can't stop underusing Erin Doherty's Anne, but they do include a scene where she argues with her husband in the car. Except we literally have no idea who he is at that point, and we never really will.

— Also, Anne is a showjumper! Cool tidbit, and her success compels Charles to stop being a glorious disappointment.

— Sarah Spencer desperately trying to steer Charles away from her sister was hilarious.

— Margaret's reaction to Elizabeth mistaking a girl with whiplash for a girl with a whip is brilliant.

— The shots when Charles shows up to the Spencer house to see Diana are nearly identical with the ones when he goes to see Sarah; it's a clever visual cue that their relationship is equally doomed.
